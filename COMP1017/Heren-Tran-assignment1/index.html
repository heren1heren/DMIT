About Hipsters

A hipster is often defined as a subcultural figure who values independent thinking, counter-culture, and non-mainstream interests. The modern hipster movement took shape in the 2000s, especially among urban, young adults. Hipsters tend to gravitate toward alternative fashion, music, art, and lifestyles that emphasize creativity and individuality. Their distinct aesthetic often includes thrift-store clothing, vintage styles, and accessories that may seem ironic or retro, signaling the rejection of mass-market consumerism.

History of Hipsterism

The origins of hipsterism can be traced back to the 1940s in the United States, where the term "hipster" was first used to describe jazz aficionados who adopted the laid-back, rebellious lifestyle of African-American jazz musicians.

These early hipsters embraced the subversive spirit of jazz, which stood in contrast to the mainstream culture of the time, and were characterized by their bohemian attitudes, relaxed fashion, and love for spontaneity. Read more about the history of hipsterism here.

Hipster Fashion

Hipster fashion is known for its eclectic mix of vintage, indie, and often sustainable styles that reflect individuality and creativity. Hipster Speak

Vintage

  Clothing or items that are from or imitate styles of past decades, often sourced from thrift stores or second-hand shops.

Artisanal

  Handcrafted, small-batch goods made with high-quality materials, often emphasizing traditional methods and sustainability.

Indie

  Short for "independent," typically referring to music, films, or fashion that is produced without major corporate backing, emphasizing originality and creativity.

Locavore

  A person who prefers eating locally sourced, organic food, often from farmers' markets or community-supported agriculture (CSA) programs.

Ironic

  A style or attitude where hipsters embrace outdated or uncool things (like retro fashion or kitschy items) in a self-aware and often humorous way.
